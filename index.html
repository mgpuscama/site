RESEARCH / INVESTIGACIÓN

My Research Interests / Mis Intereses en Investigación

I am interested in the processes that adults undergo when learning a second language (L2), and how lab-based research can inform and improve classroom practices. I study different aspects of L2 acquisition, such as vocabulary learning, semantic network development, statistical learning, and pronunciation of L2 phonetic contrasts. I also have experience studying lexical retrieval in L2 learners and heritage speakers, anticipatory processes in L2 speakers, and translation studies.

Estoy interesada en el proceso que atraviesan los adultos cuando están aprendiendo una segunda lengua (L2), y también cómo la investigación experimental puede informar y mejorar las prácticas de enseñanza. Estudio diferentes aspectos de la adquisición de L2, como aprendizaje de vocabulario, desarrollo de redes semánticas, aprendizaje estadístico y pronunciación de contrastes fonéticos en la L2. También tengo experiencia en el estudio del acceso léxico en estudiantes de L2 y hablantes de herencia, procesos anticipatorios en hablantes de L2 y estudios de traducción.

My Current Projects / Mis Proyectos Actuales

Theme vs. Co-Occurrence: How L2 Vocabulary Presentation Affects Acquisition, Storing, and Processing

Vocabulary size is one of the strongest indicators of second language (L2) proficiency (Meara, 1996). L2 learners are exposed to large amounts of vocabulary, yet they may struggle retrieving words for spontaneous communication. This difficulty may not be strictly related to vocabulary size, but rather to the lexicon organization or topology, which is crucial for language processing. Lexicon topology underlies how the lexicon is searched, how words are associated, and how easily they are retrieved (De Deyne & Storms, 2014). Thus, an effective lexicon allows for rapid word retrieval, for use in real time. Then, one of the main goals of L2 instruction should be teaching words in a way that favors meaningful semantic connections.

The connections established in the lexicon depend on vocabulary exposure. The way words are encountered affects how they are stored (Bybee, 1998; Hills et al., 2009), and if two words co-occur often, they become strongly associated in the mind, facilitating each other’s activation (Collins & Loftus, 1975). During L2 acquisition, words are often presented in thematic lists (e.g., food), favoring a lexicon organized by shared features (burger-hot dog). This approach is not wrong, because words within the same themes tend to co-occur. However, thematic lists offer a partial picture of co-occurrence. For example, beer and football do not belong strictly to the same theme (food/sports), but they co-occur frequently and are associated. If learners are only exposed to thematic lists, they must work harder to make associations to larger themes (e.g., leisure) or across co-occurring themes.

Many prominent L1 lexical semantics models are based on co-occurrence distribution (Jackson & Bolger, 2014). These models employ semantic networks to show how words interrelate, for example, in different corpora. Other researchers use word associations to tap into semantic connections in a controlled experimental setting (De Deyne & Storms, 2008). Word associations can be used a proxy for co-occurrence, as we expect that co-occurring vocabulary is strongly associated in the lexicon (Collins & Loftus, 1975). Using word associations, we can compare the semantic connections L2 learners and native speakers establish, by computing measures of connectivity (see methods). Then, we can build teaching materials that facilitate complex semantic connections. The goal is to test computer-based materials to help L2 learners build a more robust semantic network. Two experiments will be conducted: online word association (Exp. 1), and computer training with eye-tracking (Exp. 2).

Statistical learning of non-adjacent dependency in noisy environments

Click here to see a poster and preliminary results of this project

The seminal paper by Gómez (2002) focused on the acquisition of non-adjacent dependencies by adults and toddlers, using a miniature artificial grammar. Participants were able to extract statistical regularities from the input, an aXb grammar, thus learning that a and b were dependent on each other, whereas the intervening X element was variable. These results suggest that the acquisition of non-adjacent dependencies is possible both in children and adults. However, given that language learning does not occur in silence as in the lab setting, this study aims to build on Gómez (2002) and similar studies to answer the following research questions: (1) If adults are exposed to an artificial language, to what extent are they sensitive to non-adjacent dependencies presented in noisy environments?; (2) Do noise and overlapping speech hinder learning, or do they offer a desirable difficulty?; (3) What is the effect of foreign-accentedness in overlapping speech?

To test the acquisition of non-adjacent dependencies, we will use the miniature artificial grammar developed by Gómez (2002). This artificial grammar had the structure aXb, in which a and b are constant elements (non-adjacent dependency), and X is an element that varies. In this grammar, there were six monosyllabic elements forming three a-b pairs. The three a elements were pel, dak, and vot, and the three b elements were rud, tood, and jic. The X element varied between twenty-four disyllabic non-words, e.g. balip and gople.

Following Gómez (2002), two languages were constructed using these non-words, L1 and L2. In L1, the non-adjacent dependencies to be learned are pel-X-rud, dak-X-tood, and vot-X-jic. In L2, the non-adjacent dependencies are pel-X-jic, vot-X-tood, and dak-X-rud. In both languages, all dependencies will co-occur with each of the twenty-four X items listed above. Thus, a sample string for L1 would be pel balip rud, and for L2 would be pel balip jic. Each participant will be randomly assigned to one of four conditions: 1) control, 2) white noise, 3) overlapping non-accented speech, and 4) overlapping accented speech. Within each condition, half of the participants will be exposed to L1, and the other half to L2.

We hypothesize that non-adjacent dependency learning is possible in noisy environments, as attested in previous studies (Erickson & Newman, 2017; McMillan & Saffran, 2016). However, it is not clear whether learners exposed to language with background noise perform better or worse than those exposed to language in isolation. Additionally, there are no previous studies focusing on non-adjacent dependency learning in overlapping speech, thus there are no clear predictions in this regard. Overlapping speech might either hinder acquisition or pose a desirable difficulty to improve learning. The decision to include both non-accented and accented speech in the overlapping speech conditions is motivated by the diverse contexts of language acquisition faced by learners. For instance, a toddler may acquire English in the United States having a native and a non-native parent, the last one speaking accented English. The presence of overlapping speakers may be conducive to richer representations of the language (and better learning), or merely increase difficulty.

[pi.an.o] or [pja.no]? Diphthong/hiatus production in L1 English L2 Spanish learners

Click here to see a video presentation of this project

When two vowel sounds are adjacent in a word, they can either belong to the same syllable (cute [kjut]), forming a diphthong, or to separate syllables (piano [pi.'a.no]), forming a hiatus. Each language has different rules pertaining which vowels can form diphthongs. For example, looking at English and Spanish, the sequence /ia/ in English is produced as hiatus, whereas in Spanish it is typically a diphthong (viaje [bja.xe] ‘trip’) (Hualde & Prieto, 2002; Kilpatrick & Pierce, 2014). In Spanish /ia/ is a hiatus only if the /i/ is stressed (María [ma.'ɾi.a]). These differences entail a conflict for English speakers learning Spanish, because they may produce every /ia/ as hiatus, without making a diphthong/hiatus contrast in Spanish.

The research questions that motivate this project are: (1) Is there a measurable diphthong/hiatus contrast in L2 Spanish learners? (2) Does higher L2 proficiency improve pronunciation of vowel sequences?Native Spanish speakers and L2 Spanish learners completed a production task, in which they were recorded reading Spanish words with a /ia/. Vowel sequences were analyzed acoustically, using duration and formant trajectories as indicators of diphthong/hiatus contrast. Results suggest that L2 learners produced a contrast comparable to native speakers, but with longer durations overall. The analysis of formant trajectories shows that both groups produced similar formant transitions from the first to the second vowel, and that differences of duration are primarily observed in the second vowel. Furthermore, formant trajectories were modulated by Spanish proficiency and cognate status.

TEACHING / DOCENCIA

I have experience teaching language, linguistics, and other content courses in Argentina and the United States. In Mendoza, Argentina I have worked in Universidad Nacional de Cuyo and Universidad del Aconcagua, teaching introductory linguistics courses as well as production and comprehension of academic texts in Med School and Law School. In the United States I have taught at Louisiana State University and The Pennsylvania State University, courses in Spanish and Italian. Additionally, I have experience in higher ed education administration, as I worked for two years as Content Supervisor for the Spanish Basic Language Program at Penn State.

Tengo experiencia dando clases de idioma, lingüística y otros cursos de contenido en Argentina y en Estados Unidos.
